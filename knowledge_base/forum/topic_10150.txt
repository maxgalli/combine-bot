REPLY 2 (by adewit @ 2022-05-06T14:05:49.814Z):
Hi Sebastian,
You can find a little more information in slide 9 of this talk - the test statistic is indeed of the form you quote here. When you run on toys, n_i (or d_i in the slides) replaces the observed data. Note that by default the signal strength is left floating in the fit when the test statistic is evaluated.
I’m not sure I understand your third question - the expected distribution will be built up by generating the toys, based on this and the observed value you can then calculate a p-value to assess the goodness of fit - you don’t need anything else.

REPLY 4 (by adewit @ 2022-05-12T09:17:30.236Z):
Hi Sebastian,
I wouldn’t say it exactly like that: the toys are used to build up the test statistic distribution (so that you can calculate the p-value), and when evaluating the test statistic on the real data you’re calculating the test statistic between ‘model/background function’ and ‘data’
For the distribution of the test statistic values - I would expect there is something in your model that means the degree of freedom counting is not perfect (any uncertainties?). If you want to convince yourself that the test statistic distribution does follow a chi2, it is probably easier to construct a toy example with a small number of bins.
Best,
Adinda
