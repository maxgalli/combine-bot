REPLY 2 (by pkeicher @ 2025-02-19T08:12:33.079Z):
Hi Hyeon-Seo,
in principle, there can be differences between the expected and observed significance if the observed post-fit estimate of your POI and/or the associated uncertainties are different from your expectation. There are a number of reasons where such differences can come from, e.g.

differences between the simulation and data are mitigated by adjusting nuisance parameters, which can also influence the POI estimate (i.e. correlations)
you constrain (a set of) parameters much more strongly than you would have expected (might be the case depending on the analysis details and the parameters)
your estimate of the POI is different from the expectations (similar to the first point, but the change wouldn’t arise from higher-order correlations)

Additionally, imho, comparing to an older version of the analysis is of course advisable, but the findings there might not be applicable directly to your iteration. This depends obviously on the details of the analysis, but if you for example use a new MVA that is trained on (simulated) data with an updated calibration, things might be a little different. Of course, the exact effect of all these differences are hard to pin down though.
Just based on the numbers you have shared, it’s a little hard to see if things are working as they should. It’s already encouraging that there are no obvious errors in the parameter estimation, but here are a couple of other things you might want to check:

expected/observed NLL curves of the POI in the individual categories and the combination (if your analysis is fully unblinded of course, which I guess it is at this point)
look at the pulls (and maybe impacts?) in the categories and in the combination and try to see the difference between the observed and the expected constraints
compare the observed post-fit values of the nuisance parameters between the fits in the individual categories and the combination
have a look at the correlations in these fits and see if correlations between parameters change depending on which categories you use in the fit.

Maybe this will give you more insight into what is happening. If you don’t want to build individual workspaces for the categories, you can also build a combined workspace with channel masks. This will enable you to turn individual categories on and off on the fly, which can be useful for debugging purposes.
Let us know if you have any additional questions!
Cheers,
Philip

REPLY 5 (by pkeicher @ 2025-02-28T11:01:47.696Z):
Hi Hyeon-Seo,
thanks for the update. Here are a couple of thoughts to your observations:
Impacts
It’s a little hard to compare the details of the expected and observed impacts you share because of the following reasons

the set of parameters shown in your pngs is not exactly the same. Particularly for the floating parameters (in grey) things makes formulating an expectation hard, mainly because I don’t know the details of your models and what values they should have.
since you shared a screen shot and not the full pdf, I can’t look for the parameters in each version of the impact routine.

Can you perhaps share the command you used and the pdf instead of pngs? Based on the constrained parameters (in black), I agree that there aren’t any pulls or constraints to worry about. However, looking at the shapeBkg_bkg_ch5__norm parameter is interesting. Based on your FitDiagnostics command, you actually freeze this parameter in the fit - it’s mainly in the impacts because the combineTool routine submits the impact for all parameters in the workspace by default, regardless of the freezeParameters option (there is another, separate option in the combineTool to skip specific parameters btw). So looking at this too much won’t tell us a lot about your final fit in the end.
However, I noticed that this is pulled up w.r.t. the expected value for this parameter, from 15020 → 20840. To me, this might indicate that there is a surplus in data that the fit tries to mitigate by increasing the yield of processes where it’s allowed. In the normal fit, this wouldn’t be this background shape since it’s frozen, so maybe the fit uses the signal instead?
FitDiagnostics and shapes
First question: At least for me, the command you sent renders such as this: --freezeParameters pdf_index_ggh shapeBkg_bkg_ch5__norm (i.e. a space between pdf_index_ggh and shapeBkg_bkg_ch5__norm). I think this should be a comma separated list, Is that actually the case?
I must admit that I’m not an expert with the H->GammaGamma core-PDF fit method, so take the following with a grain of salt - maybe one of the other experts can chime in here. But I’m guessing that different models are parameterized with the pdf_index_ggh parameter, which fixes model parameters such as e.g. the width? I’m not sure if I understand your comment about fixing the integral of the signal model. The signal strength r is a multiplicative parameters that scales the yield (i.e. the integral) of your signal across all fit categories. So even if the nominal integral is fixed, the signal strength is still floating and can increase the yield, right?
The shapes you shared show exectly this increase in yield - based on the impact plots, you increase the signal yield by a factor of 2.6 (if there aren’t any additional parameters that scale the signal yield of course). It would be interesting to see the data points in your plot - based on the post-fit values, I’d guess that the MC undershoots the data, which is why the fit mitigates by increasing the yield of the processes.
I think comparing the MC and data yields and shapes in the individual categories might be a good next step to take (assuming you’re far along in the unblinding procedure of course). Let us know how it goes!
Cheers,
Philip
