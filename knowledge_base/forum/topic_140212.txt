QUESTION (by mpriyada @ 2025-12-18T10:51:51.673Z):
Dear Combine experts,
I am using the Combine framework to extract the signal strength in a template-based analysis, where both signal and background are provided as binned templates.
The signal process under study is
g d→tW, (W decaying leptonically)
which is allowed in the Standard Model but is strongly suppressed due to its dependence on Vtd​. As a result, the expected signal yield after luminosity weighting is extremely small compared to the dominant backgrounds.
The templates are constructed as follows:
A BDT is trained to separate the signal from the dominant backgrounds  (ttbar and tW coming from gb→tW ). The BDT output is used to build a 2D discriminant distribution, which is then adaptively binned such that the lumi-weighted sum of signal events is approximately equal in all bins. This adaptively binned 2D distribution is subsequently unrolled into a 1D histogram, which is used as the final signal and background templates in the fit.
At this stage of the study, no systematic uncertainties are included. I am also sharing the corresponding datacard, together with the signal and background templates used as input.
To extract the upper limit, I run the following command:
“combine -M HybridNew datacard.txt -t -1 --expectSignal 1 --LHCmode LHC-limits”
When performing the fit using the HybridNew method, I observe that it fails to determine a stable upper limit on the signal strength. This appears to be driven by the very low signal-to-background ratio in the templates.
I have also tried the Asymptotic method, which does converge, but it results in a very large upper limit on the signal strength (of order (10^3) ).
My questions are:

How should one properly deal with situations where the signal is orders of magnitude smaller than the background and HybridNew is unable to set a stable limit?
Is it acceptable to rely on the Asymptotic limits in such a case, even if the resulting upper

limit is very large, or is it strongly recommended to use HybridNew only?
3. Are there recommended strategies to improve the stability of the limit extraction in this regime?
Any guidance would be greatly appreciated.
Best regards,
Manaswini Priyadarshini

REPLY 3 (by mpriyada @ 2025-12-19T12:15:57.477Z):
Dear Giacomo,
Thank you for the clarification. I would like to confirm my understanding of the signal scaling and how the extracted signal strength should be used for the determination of |Vtd|.
In my analysis, I extract |Vtd| using the relation:
Vtd_observed = sqrt(signal strength) × Vtd_SM
I observe the following behavior depending on how the signal is treated in the datacard:
When I introduce a scaling parameter
Signal_scale rateParam ch1 Signal 1000 [1000,1000],
the fit returns a signal strength of
r < 1.59 ± 0.061.
Without explicit signal scaling (using only –rmax 5000), the fit instead gives
r < 676 ± 6.26.
If I directly use r = 676 in the above relation, I obtain
|Vtd| ~ O(10⁻¹), which is significantly larger than the Standard Model expectation.
On the other hand, using r = 1.59 gives
|Vtd| ~ O(10⁻²), which appears much more reasonable and closer to expectations.
This leads to my main confusion. The value r = 1.59 is obtained after scaling the signal by a factor of 1000 in the datacard. In that case, when extracting Vtd_observed, should I:
directly use r = 1.59, or
interpret the effective signal strength as r_eff = 1.59 × 1000 = 1590 before applying
Vtd_observed = sqrt(signal strength) × Vtd_SM?
In other words, I am unsure whether the scaling factor applied in the datacard should be explicitly unfolded when translating the fitted signal strength into a physical Vtd value.
I would greatly appreciate your guidance on which signal strength definition is the correct one to use for the final Vtd extraction.
Best regards,
Manaswini

REPLY 4 (by gortona @ 2025-12-19T13:25:33.000Z):
Dear Manaswini,
In your case, since you scale your signal by a factor of 1000, the actual value you should use is 1590.
Basically you should either get 1590 (not scaling) or 1.59 (scaling), but these are actually the same quantity, you are just scaling it differently.
As I said, given your inputs a signal strength of O(1000) is not unreasonable. If this is very far from your expectation my first suspect would be that maybe some input cross-sections was not correct, or that you are mixing fb and pb somewhere?
Regardless of the cross-section, the HybridNew and the Asymptotic strategies should give you very similar - almost identical - results. Can you share the datacards and commands you are using? Also, do yo get any warning or error when running hybridNew?
Best,
Giacomo
