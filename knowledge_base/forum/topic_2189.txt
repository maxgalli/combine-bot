Right, exactly.

Following this, one would expect the difference in exclusion limits when
comparing CLs and CLs+b criteria to be mostly at the lowest limits,
i.e., exclusion at 2.5% and 16% confidence level, while the limits at
84% and 97.5% should not change much. And really, looking at the numbers

limits at 2.5% is almost five times (CLs+b gives 5 times lower limits
than CLs+b!), while at 97.5%, the relative difference is 28%. The

Yes. I think this pattern in the quantiles of the expected limits between CLs+b and CLs is normal.

All this then leads to the observed limit going from +2sigma (up from
the expected limit) up to +2.35 sigma with the switch to CLs+b.

How did you determine this is 2.35 sigma? The expected limits give you the [0,025, 0.16, 0.5, 0.84, 0.95] quantiles, but you seem to be making some extra step to go from the observed limit into a p-value, and then a z score, that is not entirely clear to me.

However,
the Significance test returns 2.95 sigma significance. Therefore, event
with CLs+b, there is a mismatch between the limits and the significance.
Lower than when using CLs, but it's still there.
Could there be "statistical" reasons why the two tests, CLs(+b) and
Significance, may give different significances?

The limit and significance are not the same test (different null hypotheses), and while under some specific conditions there might be a correspondence, it will not be true in general (e.g. to the extent the asymptotic approximation holds).

If so, which result should be quoted as the observed significance? The
one from the limits or the one from the Significance test?

If the null hypothesis is background-only, then we would quote the number from the significance test.
