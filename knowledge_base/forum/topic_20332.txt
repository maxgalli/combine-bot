Dear combine experts:
As part of my in development thesis, I am attempting to write up some of the math used by physicists (and in particular combine, since we use it too) to actually get from out input histograms and datacards to the limits we plan to set once we unblind our analysis. I’ve been through various backgrounds looking into this, from pdg statistics refreshers to the theses of my former peers to classic references such as https://cds.cern.ch/record/1379837.

REPLY 5 (by kcormier @ 2023-02-15T17:16:38.524Z):
Hi Brooks,
I am not sure if I am interpreting your understanding correctly or not. However, I think there may be a little misunderstanding. In particular, its not clear to me what exactly you mean by “is that shape used to compute a bin by bin gaussian distribution that is collectively optimized across all bins for a single pull value?”; but I suspect there may be some misunderstanding here.
The external constraint term itself does not directly “know anything about” the bins of the distributions. i.e. for a given point in model space, the value of the constraint term of the likelihood depends only on the values of the nuisance parameters themselves, and is independent of any binned values. This is the gaussian term referred to in Pietro’s second reference, and is also the  green “pe” term called “NP constraints” on the s.3 of the physics days talk Jan Lukas linked. This term can be a Gaussian, but need not be. In Combine, for example it is gaussian for some uncertainty types (such as LnN and shape), but this is not always the case (for e.g. Gamma uncertainties).
Of course there is a connection between the constraint term and the bin values. This is defined by the model, where the expectation depends on the values of the nuisance parameters. Mathematically this is shown by the theta vector appearing in the Poisson term “Exp events per bin” in light blue on s.3 of the talk.
The important distinction I would like to make is that the form of the constraint term does not itself define how the model expectation is derived from the values of parameters (both signal and nuisance parameters). The way that shape systematics effect the expected value of the model for a given value of a nuisance parameter is shown on s.3 above and explained  under the “template shape uncertainties” section of the “binned shape analysis” section of the combine documentation [1]. It involves a few steps, including separating our the shape and normalization effects and some interpolation to deal with asymmetric up- and down- shift values.
What is optimized in the end is the full likelihood, which includes the poisson term quantifying the agreement of the data with the expectation (which can be evaluated per bin), and the nuisance parameter constraint terms (which do not include any information about bins) which quantify the agreement with externally provided constraints.
I hope that helps to clarify.
Best,
Kyle
[1]  https://cms-analysis.github.io/HiggsAnalysis-CombinedLimit/part2/settinguptheanalysis/#binned-shape-analysis

** In fact you only provide three points (nominal, up shift, and down shift), but the change of the process is a continuous function of the nuisance parameter, and so there has to be some way in which those three points are translated into a full continuous function of the nuisance parameter.
