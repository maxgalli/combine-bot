QUESTION (by vischia @ 2021-11-04T09:06:36.000Z):
Hi Byeonghak,
combine supports out of the box one-sided shape variations: given your nominal template, you can simply put your one-sided uncertainty shape as e.g. systUp, and the nominal again as the other (e.g. systDn, in this example). Combine will then build a pdf that allows only one-sided variations.
This is also true for lnN uncertainties, you just put e.g. 1/1.03 to have a one-sided variation.
So, if the prescription for that uncertainty is to have it one-sided, this is the way of telling combine to do that.
I hope this helps!
Cheers,
Pietro

REPLY 3 (by vischia @ 2021-11-04T09:28:53.000Z):
Hi Adinda,
I think fit problems are most often due to the unreasonable amount of nuisance parameters and bins we are plugging into numerical-minimization-based fits nowadays, rather than just having one-sided pdfs.
Besides, if conceptually a variation is one-sided, then any solution different from having it one-sided is just a hack that does something different from the intended behaviour of that uncertainty. The other proper way would be to mandate that the people who define the prescription for each uncertainty avoid one-sided prescriptions, but that is not always possible (if a systematic is intrinsically one-sided, there’s not much one can do).

Hi all,
Sorry, no, the one sided variation is not correct. NNPDF3.1 is
explicitly a set with symmetric uncertainties. The correct procedure is
indeed what you mention at first: build the variation for central +
abs(shift) and central - abs(shift). Or, slightly more accurately, take
the percent variation. If you have a histogram "cen" for the central
variation and histograms "var" made with each of the 100 weights, the
100 nuisances can be formed as
up = var if var > cen else cen*cen/var down = var if var < cen else
cen*cen/var
As for studies that take half the variations as up and half as down,
this is specific to the PDF. Many other PDF sets besides NNPDF (CTEQ,
MMHT, etc.) do indeed distribute the variations this way, and then the
uncertainty is computed by taking the variation histograms directly from
the corresponding weights and it is not required to be symmetric.
Best,
Kenneth

REPLY 8 (by kelong @ 2021-11-04T11:35:47.000Z):

Hi Byeonghak,
I'm not sure if I understand what you wrote. Each of the 100 variations
of the NNPDF3.1 set give an alternative distribution for your
observable. They are orthogonal Hessian uncertainties, so deriving a
total uncertainty from them involves taking the sum in quadrature as you
know. If you instead want to treat them as individual uncertainties and
profile them in the fit, you need to just take the histograms themselves
as the uncertainties band, the same way you would do for an uncertainty
like the lepton efficiency uncertainty, there is no sum involved. The
only catch is that this is just one side of the uncertainty, and you
need to symmeterize it.
Technically, the samples have 100 gen weights. You then make 100
histograms weighted by each of these weights w_i, hist_var =
Fill(variable, w_i). This histogram amounts to an up variation if it is
greater than nominal, and a down variation if it is less. Of course it
could also have a shape in a distribution that isn't always greater or
smaller, in which case the difference between up and down is arbitrary,
the important thing is that you make an uncertainty band out of the
histogram wrt the nominal.
The closest thing to a reference to this I know of is the MC2Hessian
paper which tells how the Hessian sets are obtained in the special case
of NNPDF (which general has MC replica sets):
https://arxiv.org/pdf/1505.06736.pdf
It doesn't really concern profiling the individual sets, but it's no
different than the way you treat any Hessian uncertainty if you want to
profile it.
Best,
Kenneth
