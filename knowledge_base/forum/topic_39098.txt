REPLY 3 (by nckw @ 2024-04-16T12:51:00.984Z):
Hi,
In addition to checking what happens with more Toys, you can also explicitly check the distributions of the test statistic for each value of the signal strength (see https://cms-analysis.github.io/HiggsAnalysis-CombinedLimit/latest/part3/commonstatsmethods/#plotting and scroll down to the part about the test/plotTestStatCLs.py script). If you see very jagged behaviour, then this can be the cause of the behaviour in the bands you see.
Now, if you have extremely small backgrounds and you do see the jagged behaviour, it could simply be that the procedure used to calculate the limits is not appropriate. We have an alternative method which behaves better for small yields, which was most recently used in the \tau\to3\mu search - you can find a description of that here : https://indico.cern.ch/event/1284926/#13-cousins-highland-in-zero-ba . This may not be necessary if your test statistic distributions look ok

REPLY 12 (by nckw @ 2024-04-22T10:54:36.323Z):
Hi Wren,
Right, its just the way the toys are handled thats different. You can certainly run this method in combine. You just need to remove the option --LHC-model LHC-limits and replace it with --generateNuisances=1 --generateExternalMeasurements=0 --fitNuisances=1 --testStat LHC
The subtlety about the flat priors in the \tau \to 3\mu analysis is really only important if you have “free floating” parameters in your model (i.e things other than log-normals, or param type nuisances). for small yields, one can argue that the major effect is from the background normalisation but you could produce flat priors for any/all such parameters. Note that in combine, we only talk about priors on parameters so if the background yield is fixed, there’s no prior but if you usually let it float (eg rateParam or similar), then you should include a flat prior for that in this setup. Let me know, as there is an extra step in this case to get things to work properly.
Best,
Nick

REPLY 18 (by nckw @ 2024-04-23T15:07:32.928Z):
Hi Wren,
Thanks for checking and sending the commands + datacard. Indeed, nothing you’ve written seems wrong to me.
I think given what you see here, we really are stuck into the discrete nature of Poisson statistics. Bascially, each of these spikes corresponds to a specific number of events that gets generated (0,1,2) which yields a specific value of the test statistic. The method you tried here can sometimes help to smoothen out the distribution since generating this way convolutes the Poisson with the log-normal terms but given the size of the systematics you have, it won’t make a huge effect (which is what you see here)
I don’t think there’s more that can be, or indeed should be done as I think you have essentially proven here that for your analysis, the distribution of the test statistic is discrete (and not due to lack of toys or the way in which the toys are generated) and so there will necessarily be jumps in the bands due to this. My only piece of advice then would be to not try to connect the bands when you draw the brazil plot since it could be misleading for points inbetween those that you calculate the limit for (if that makes sense).
Thanks again for the detailed checks, feel free to pick this thread up again later in the review in case things change or if there are questions during the review about it.
All the best,
Nick
