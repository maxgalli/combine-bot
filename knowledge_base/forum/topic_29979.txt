REPLY 2 (by kcormier @ 2023-10-04T10:11:00.717Z):
Dear Long,
Sorry for the delayed response. To your first question, I think a useful thing to do is to scale your process such that  a signal strength of O(1) is a meaningful value. This will tend to work better from the perspective of the minimizer and also for some arguments (such as the accuracy) which chose reasonable default values based on the assumption that signal strengths of O(1) are the relevant numerical range.
In the datacard you’ve given, the statistics are quite limited, with the background expectation being only 0.09 events. So its not surprising that you might get different results from asymptotic methods, which require a few conditions to hold, and the HybridNew results which don’t rely on those assumptions. On the other hand, the interval you are showing for the HybridNew result looks suspicious to me, and when I ran a quick test for one point, the interval seemed larger.
It might be worth making the change I suggested, and perhaps also checking some of the plots and debugging mentioned in the relevant section of the documentation [1].
Please let us know if that helps or if there are still issues.
Cheers,

REPLY 4 (by kcormier @ 2023-10-05T12:19:10.048Z):
Hi Long,
Yes, I just checked quickly after scaling up the signal.
When you say much larger “signal and background yields” and you referring to signal+background?  Whether or not the asymptotic approximation will hold well for the limit depends on the background yields, but not on the signal normalization, since in the limit it is a freely floating parameter in setting the limit and the expected limit will be determined by the background.
I am not sure if what what you show from the additional datacard is in line with it being an issue with the asymptotic approximation. Since the relevant quantity is the background yield (since the signal is anyway a free parameter), and in the datacard here you expected about 3.5 events. For many purposes the asymptotic approximation starts working well with a few events like that, and it may be that what you’re seeing is some residual difference from the approximation.
I will try to take a closer look later to see if I can see anything else which might be causing issues.

REPLY 10 (by kcormier @ 2023-10-20T13:34:02.219Z):
Hi Long,
Thanks for running the tests, I think that’s helpful in seeing whats going on.
Firstly, given that for this datacard, the expected background in each bin is < 1, its not surprising that the asymptotic results give different results from the ones where you estimate the test statistics from the toys. I don’t think the asymptotic approximation is a good choice in this case, and I think it would be better to make sure that the estimate from toys is working properly.
Taking a look at the datacard and the plots you’ve made, I think that since your background-only hypothesis has >50% chance of observing no events, there is almost nothing to separate the 0.025, 0.16 and 0.5 quantiles and it makes sense that those limit expectations are very close to each other.
A bit more pedagogical walk through looking at those plots is below, in case you find it helpful.
Best,
Kyle
It is a little surprising that the 1-sigma and 2-sigma bands for the low side of the expected limit are so close to each other and to the median. But I think this might just be an effect of CLs in this very low statistics regime; Its good that you made the plots, which as far as I can tell all seem to be healthy.
For example, what you’ve shown for the 0.975 point looks reasonable to me, and from your tests it seems like you have enough statistics in the scan. Back of the envelope, with that value of signal strength modifier (2.16) you are expecting a little more than 5 signal events, whereas in this region, you expect about 0.3 background events. So I suppose that the peaks in the test statistic around 6 are when you have one observed event somewhere. For the background distribution the relative size of the first peak around ten, and peak around 6 should then be roughly the probability mass function of poisson(0.3) for 0 and 1, which as about 70/30, so it seems reasonable.
The peaks towards lower values of the test statistic seem to corresponding with the discrete observations of (1,2, … ) events in various bins.
From the plots on your /eos/ space, its good to look at the distributions for the 0.025, 0.16 and 0.5 intervals, since these are the suspicious ones values. I think to some extent this also shows whats going on, though next time if you run plotTestStatCLs.py with the arguments --expected -q <quantile_expected> you can see it even more easily (this will replace the “observed” value in the plot with the expected value).
I’ve made an example for the 0.16 point, attached below. What the method is doing is finding the 0.16 point in the expected background distribution, then trying to find the value of the signal strength such that for this value of the observed test statistic the CLs value would be 0.05.
Now you can see the 0.16 point of the background hypothesis more clearly (though you can also use the yellow and green bands to tell you the intervals).  What you can see is that the 0.025 (rightmost yellow band edge), 0.16 (“expected” line) and 0.5 (dashed vertical line) all occupy a very small range of the test statistic. The background-only distribution is concentrated there because you expect so few background events, and under the background hypothesis you usually expect to observe exactly 0 events. Moreover, the shape of both the signal+background and background-only distributions are very similar there. Since they are very similar, the CLs value (being the ratio of the integrals) barely changes as you change the expected point, and as a result also the signal strength only has to change very little in going from 0.025, to 0.16 to 0.5 of the background expectation to have a CLs of 0.05 at each of those points. But this all basically comes back to the fact that that part of the distribution is with 0 observed events and thats what you expect > 50% of the time from your background.
