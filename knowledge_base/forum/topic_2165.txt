Here, extractUnc.py prints, for example, file.Get("fit_mdf").find("r").getErrorHi() (and [...].getErrorLo()), and the printed values are set to be sigma_{tot}, sigma_{exp.+theory frozen} and sigma_{all frozen}, respectively (here I'm abusing the notations of + error and - error).
Also, [...] in the second combine run is the list of the nuisance parameters for all experimental+theory uncertainties which is separated by ',', e.g., jer,jes,lumi,isrttbar (of course it could be the name of group of uncertainty, like exp,theory, with --freezeNuisanceGroups).
And then,
sigma_{exp.+theory}^2    = sigma_{tot}^2                - sigma_{exp.+theory frozen}^2
sigma_{MC sample size}^2 = sigma_{exp.+theory frozen}^2 - sigma_{all frozen}^2
sigma_{stat.}^2          = sigma_{all frozen}^2.
This is the procedures of the breakdown from my understanding. But I'm not sure if it is correct. Could you tell me if there is a problem on this?
3. And also it seems like there is another way to calculate sigma_{MC sample size}^2:
sigma_{MC sample size}^2 = sigma_{tot}^2 - sigma_{autoMCStat frozen}^2,
where sigma_{autoMCStat frozen} is obtained by the following command:
# ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
combine \-M MultiDimFit \-d ws\_saved\.root \-n MLUnfold \-\-snapshotName MultiDimFit \-\-saveFitResult \-\-freezeParameters autoMCStats
python3 extractUnc.py multidimfitMLUnfold.root  # It extracts the uncertainty from the fit, \sigma_{autoMCStat frozen}
# ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
I'm not sure which way is right. Or, does it depend on my strategy(?) of the uncertainty breakdown?
4. I'm not sure about which category of uncertainties (systematic uncertainty vs. statistical uncertainty) 'MC sample size' is in. Although it is the uncertainty from the "MC statistical uncertainty", it seems like it should be regarded as a systematic one because it is about the model, not about the real data. I also found that some studies classified it as a systematic uncertainty. But I'm still not sure about that.

Dear Byeonghak Ko,
The uncertainty breakdown method in the slides you linked is what we
usually do in the Higgs PAG. There are of course other ways to get an
idea of the contribution of different (groups of) uncertainties, so let
me start by saying that whatever you do, it's important to be clear in
the documentation of your analysis about what was actually done. If you
want to quote a measurement Â± uncertainty and break that uncertainty
down into a few groups, then the method in the slides is reasonable
because adding the different components in quadrature returns the total
uncertainty, which is not always guaranteed with other methods.
The procedure you wrote down in 2) looks correct, at least as far as the
successive freezing and quadratic subtraction is concerned. You will
however have to do the full likelihood scan (in any case we don't
recommend quoting the uncertainties from the covariance matrix for a
measured quantity).
The procedure in 3) also works (you will need --freezeNuisanceGroups,
though). You may get a different result than in 2) because there are
correlations between the fitted parameters. That also means that if you
do something like this for all your groups of systematics, adding them
in quadrature might not give you the same answer as the total
uncertainty.
About 4): right, it is a systematic uncertainty (when we say
"statistical uncertainties" that only relates to the size of the data
sample, they are uncertainties that can only be reduced by improving the
signal/background separation or collecting more data).
Best,
Adinda

REPLY 5 (by adewit @ 2021-09-20T21:05:46.000Z):
*** Discussion title: Higgs Combination
Dear Byeonghak Ko,
Just to be clear, if you follow the procedure as in your point 2) the
sum in quadrature of the different components will give you the total
uncertainty, while this is not necessarily the case if you only freeze a
single set of uncertainty sources at a time (that is, if you are not
freezing successively) - ie what you had in point 3.
I don't think there's any way to define a correlation matrix between
different groups of parameters, but you can of course show the
correlation matrix between the individual parameters. The idea that
correlations between parameters mean sigma_tot =
sqrt(sigma_a^2+sigma_b^2) no longer holds should be familiar to most
people, consider for example basic error propagation formulae. You could
remind people who are not happy with the difference of this.
Best,
Adinda
