REPLY 12 (by tihsu @ 2025-09-18T16:37:09.613Z):
Hi Andrea,
Sorry if our slide was unclear. Just as a follow-up and to clarify our approach: our results are based on DNNs optimized for different mass points. When calculating the global significance, we use only the DNN corresponding to the mass point of interest (e.g. 600 GeV here). The toys are generated under a background-only hypothesis using -- saveToys --expectSignal=0 —toysFrequentist.
To extract the maximum significance across different mass points, we prepared the signal template by applying the 600 GeV DNN to all mass points (i.e. taking inputs from signal sample directly, applying all the correction and feed into the network.) This ensures that the mathematical treatment is consistent across the scan, and that nuisance effects are handled in the same way. We think the correlation should be taken care in this treatment.
The fit is performed simultaneously across all our signal regions (as shown in slides 4–7), which leads to about O(50) bins in the final fit.
Please let us know if any part remains unclear, or if you have suggestions for improvement. Thank you.
Best,
Ting-Hsiang for the team

REPLY 13 (by amarini @ 2025-09-18T16:37:36.874Z):
Hi Efe,
nowhere in your procedure you describe how a toy should be correlated in the different dnns.
You may also have effects induced by systematic constraints, but the procedure should be more or less fine in that regard, if standard checks are performed. You are testing 9 mass points, so your global pvalue, should be bounded between (plocal=2.4s)
1.46 < p_{global} < 2.4 \sigma
(9 bins independent vs 9 bins fully correlated). If I have to guess, you are likely around 1.8-2.0s.
Differences with respect to what you described
For instance, if you have a fluctuations in dnn bin6 of the 200 GeV model, it should be highly correlated with a hypothetical dnn bin6 of a 201 GeV model, modestly correlated with a 300 or 400 GeV model (depending on the resolution of the signal), and negligibly correlated with a 1 TeV model.
Simple Example
If you think to have a simple continuum invariant mass specturm binned in 1GeV from 100 GeV to 1 TeV, you generate a single toy for the full spectrum, and the fit will take to account for the fluctuations (through the signal resolution). I.e. if your experimental+theory resolution is 1 GeV, the model 100GeV will be significantly correlated with 101, but negligibly with >110 GeV. If your resolution is 100 GeV, you will have that the 200 GeV point is still influenced by a 100 GeV fluctuation. Now, (in this trivial example) a dnn will just shuffle the bins, accordingly to their likelihood (prop to S/B), given the specific signal model. (with more or less flat background), this means that you are more or less counting in number of (resolutions) sigmas around the nominal value. In this case, you would still generate the toy as above, but when reshuffling and rebinning, you will observe upward fluctuations in high score bins of close by models, and negligible ones (~0) for very distant (in mass vs resolution) models.
Realistic case
Your dnn is probably more complex than what I described above, but fundamentals are the same.
Let me start saying that no magic wand exists. I need to think a bit more to what we do w/ postfit nuisance, because the resampling there is likely different.
What I would try to assess would be the following:

Create a vector (histogram) of unrolled dnn bins [dnn200-bin1, -bin2 … dnn900-bin6] … (i.e. all possible observations).
Derive from MC the covariance matrix of it, possibly with resampling techniques (like JN, resampling w/ or w/o reinsertion, ecc…).
[ need to think about it], Probably fit one distribution b-only and use it to generate postfit toys (gaussian) with the covariance matrix above. I would also generate the global observables in this case.
Chunk the toy in each dnn, and fit with that model (with the global observables as usual for the postfit toys).
Take the maximum significance for each toy.
Use this to report the global significance.

Cheers,
-Andrea
