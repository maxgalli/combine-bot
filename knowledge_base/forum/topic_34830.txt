REPLY 4 (by kcormier @ 2024-02-19T13:21:35.623Z):
Dear Xingchen,
I’m not quite sure I understand what you mean when you say all the models are post-fit?
I think that Adinda’s point in asking the question about the pre-fit expected significance is that your expectation of the combined expected significance being the sum in quadrature of the individual ones is true for some models. But even in those cases, the observed significance will not be the sum in quadrature of the four observed significances, because each channel will have independent data, and the observed values will almost certainly not be the same across all channels – This is I think the same point you are making in your last bullet.
Combine actually provides a method for checking the fit in each of the channels against each other, it is the channel compatability test [1]. There a nominal fit is performed, using all channels, as well as a fit where the signal strength in each of the channels is allowed to float independently of the others. Then you can see the extent to which tension in the different preferred signal strengths of different channels might be affecting the overall fit.
Best,
Kyle
[1] https://cms-analysis.github.io/HiggsAnalysis-CombinedLimit/part3/commonstatsmethods/#channel-compatibility

I’m just trying to understand if the observed data is being used at any point. A more pedagogical explanation of why this matters is given with technical details below.
One should not interpret the results of the channel compatibility check as a significance. There are both physics and statistical reasons for this. The physics reasons are that it is often not physically meaningful to fully decorrelate parameters which represent the same physical effect. the statistical reason is that the significance depends on how you define your test-statistic and the number of additional parameters in the s vs s+b model, as well as a few other things, so one cannot simply change the model without reconsidering these factors. The channel compatibility check is just as the name implies: a check of how compatible the different channels are with each other. Though sometimes it is tempting, it is a mistake to interpret these values as significances.
Best,
Kyle
Some Pedagogical/Technical details on the sum-in-quadrature expectation, and when it holds
The important point is how the expectation that the significance can be given as a sum-in-quadrature of the individual significances can be justified.
If you are using the asymptotic significance calculation, (from the -M Significance method in combine) it is using the modified discovery test statistic from the “CCGV” paper [1] (see eq. 12). The test statistic \tilde{q}_0 is 0 if the best fit value is <= 0, and otherwise is the ln likelihood ratio:
\tilde{q}_0 = -2 \mathrm{ln}(\frac{\mathcal{L}(r=0)}{\mathcal{L}(r=\hat{r})})
The asymptotic distribution of the test-statistic is also worked out in the paper, and it is shown that for this asymptotic case, the significance, Z is just given by \sqrt{\tilde{q}_0}.
So for your sum-in-quadrature expectation to be correct, what you need is that the likelihood factorizes, because in that case:
\sqrt{\sum_\mathrm{bin} Z^2_\mathrm{bin}} = \sqrt{\sum_\mathrm{bin}\tilde{q}_0^{\mathrm{bin}}} = \sqrt{ -2 \mathrm{ln}(\prod_\mathrm{bin}\frac{\mathcal{L}^\mathrm{bin}(r=0)}{\mathcal{L}^\mathrm{bin}(r=\hat{r}_\mathrm{bin})})}
If the likelihood factorizes and \hat{r} is identical for all bins and for the combined fit,  then the products of the likelihoods on the individual bins are the same as the full likelihood, and your expectation for the sum-in-quadrature should hold for the asymptotic calculation.
But notice a very important point: Even if the likelihood factorizes,  \hat{r} will in general be different for each bin, and these will also in general be different than the value you get from the simultaneous fit of all bins! The expected value will be the same for each of them if you have an unbiased estimator, but as soon as you start fitting to any observed data you will change that.
[1] https://arxiv.org/abs/1007.1727

REPLY 8 (by kcormier @ 2024-02-22T10:37:10.184Z):
Dear Xingchen,
By “What you need is the likelihood factorizes” what I meant was, that for your expectation to hold, you would like to have that the full likelihood is the product of the likelihoods for each bin. Then the Right Hand Side of the equation I wrote out above is equal to the (asymptotic approximation of the) significance of the full fit. More precisely, what you need is:
\frac{\mathcal{L}( r = 0)}{\mathcal{L}(r=\hat{r})} = \prod_\mathrm{bin} \frac{\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{bin}}(r = 0 )}{\mathcal{L}^\mathrm{bin}( r = \hat{r}_\mathrm{bin})}
Note that in general, this is not the case. In particular if there are any constraint terms shared across bins then they will not factorize, and if any of the \hat{r}_\mathrm{bin} values are not equal to the best fit value from the full fit, \hat{r}, then the factorization won’t hold.
So about the use of the “observed data”, maybe this is not the most accurate term, but the important point is whether or not whatever (pseudo)data is being used in that fit has any variation (such as statistical fluctuations around the expectation) which can lead to the best fit model parameters in each bin being different from each other, which it sounds like there is in your case, if I’ve understood correctly.
Best,
Kyle
