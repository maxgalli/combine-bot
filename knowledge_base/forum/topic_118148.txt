QUESTION (by eipearso @ 2025-03-12T16:02:28.185Z):
Dear combine experts,
We are experiencing a strange problem while trying to produce impact plots for our analysis. We are conducting a search where we consider many different signal mass hypotheses in 100 GeV increments, and where our expected background yields are quite small at higher masses (~0.5 events). We need to produce impact plots fitting against data to study the effects of our nuisance parameters against the data, but without looking at the data itself since we are still blinded. The problem is that the fit fails for our impact plot for signal mass 1600 GeV in the --doInitialFit step, while those for the nearby masses all succeed. The expected background yields and systematics are similar for these masses, although the signal yields do change somewhat (~12 for 1500 GeV, ~7 for 1600 GeV and ~4 for 1700 GeV). Additionally, when we fit to Asimov toys instead of data we see that the impact plots for mass 1600 GeV come out fine. We have been unable to determine what it is about 1600 that causes it to fail while the others succeed, so do you have any suggestions on how to continue to troubleshoot this?
Very important note: We are still blinded, so we should not be looking at the data yields yet. Our question is whether or not this is a technical problem which we can continue to debug without looking at the data.
We are running like:
1500:
combineTool.py -v3 -M Impacts -d workspace1500.root -m 1500 --doInitialFit --robustFit 1 --rMin -1 --setParameters signalScaleParam=0.24536132812500003 --freezeParameters signalScaleParam --trackParameters signalScaleParam
1600:
combineTool.py -v3 -M Impacts -d workspace1600.root -m 1600 --doInitialFit --robustFit 1 --rMin -1 --setParameters signalScaleParam=0.4266357421875 --freezeParameters signalScaleParam --trackParameters signalScaleParam
1700:
combineTool.py -v3 -M Impacts -d workspace1700.root -m 1700 --doInitialFit --robustFit 1 --rMin -1 --setParameters signalScaleParam=0.6884765625 --freezeParameters signalScaleParam --trackParameters signalScaleParam
A note about the signalScaleParam: the purpose of this is to rescale the signal yields such that our median expected limits will be set around r=1 where combine is most stable. We apply the same scale factors here.
Here is the link to a cernBox folder with datacards for masses 1500, 1600 and 1700 GeV:
https://cernbox.cern.ch/s/QEeVypj1saPCIkB
Also in the folder are likelihood scan plots generated with MultiDimFit --algo grid like:
combine -M MultiDimFit [workspace].root -v2 --setParameters signalScaleParam=[scale factor] --trackParameters signalScaleParam --freezeParameters signalScaleParam --algo grid --saveFitResult --rMin -1 --points=1000 --mass [mass]
The 1500 and 1700 GeV results were plotted with combine’s plot1DScan.py , while the 1600 GeV result was plotted in a script that I wrote myself, as plot1DScan does not draw the plot if it can’t find a best fit for r.
Thank you,
Emma Pearson

REPLY 2 (by pkeicher @ 2025-03-13T09:35:59.245Z):
Hi Emma,
it’s known that the fit to data can bring its own challenges, which might also be different depending on the exact details of the observables you use in the fit and the analysis itself. So it is possible for a fit to not converge for one point even though it works fine for others (although this is unfortunate of course).
The doInitialFit step is essentially a MultiDimFit that you perform to data. If the fit does not converge, you might need to try out different minimizer options to stabilize the fit, see e.g. the docs on generic minimizer options and fit options. For example, you could try out different minimizer tolerances and strategies, and/or try a larger range for the signal strength parameter. Note that if you find a combination of options that works, it might be worthwhile to study the effect of these options on fits in mass points that already work - ideally, the results shouldn’t depend on these settings too much. It’s generally advisable to use a set of options that is as similar as possible for all the fits you perform to ensure good comparability.
Thank you for sharing the NLL scans. Just as a side note, since you stressed that you are still blinded - looking at these plots will probably also give an idea about your final result (assuming that the target of the measurement is the signal strength), so it might be wise to scrutinize these further when you’re fully unblinded.
Cheers,
Philip
P.S.: Not really a problem, but why do you track (i.e. save in the final TTre on disc) a parameter that you freeze? The value will always be constant, right?

REPLY 5 (by eipearso @ 2025-04-07T11:30:25.271Z):
Another update:
Based on advice we received from an in-person meeting with a couple of experts, I did a study using MultiDimFit with background-only toys which is detailed in the attached slides [1]. From this I gather that in the case of 0 observed events fitting is very unstable, and the choice of --rMin seems to have a large impact on whether or not the fit succeeds. I found that --rMin -0.175 (or more positive) and the default rMax of 20 had a 100% success rate for 100,000 toys.
I then attempted to use the same settings to fit to data and make the impact plot, and found that they do not work. However, by reducing the r range to --rMin -0.14 --rMax 2 (or possible observed data yields from 0 to 6 events, as noted in the slides) I do get the fits to succeed and produce the attached impact plot [2]. However, we are a bit uncertain as to whether or not this is a reasonable result. We observe that it is one-sided, but we also notice that the impacts are in the opposite direction to what we obtained when we set --rMin to 0 (see previous update), and we are not sure what to make of this.
Right now I have only studied this for our 1600 GeV mass point. The datacard [3] and workspace [4] are attached.
Thanks,
Emma

combineTool.py -v3 -M Impacts -d datacard1600.root -m 1600 --doInitialFit --robustFit 1 --rMin -0.14 --rMax 2 --setParameters signalScaleParam=0.4266357421875 --freezeParameters signalScaleParam --trackParameters signalScaleParam

REPLY 9 (by kcormier @ 2025-04-13T10:24:56.940Z):
Hi Emma,
Given that you expect <1 event, it makes sense that you start to see fit problems when you allow negative signal strengths. Negative expected yields break the Poisson model, and cause various fit failures.
While it usually makes sense to run impacts allowing negative signal strength, I don’t think that is the case here, as you are running into regions of you model which are not just “unphysical” but outside of the mathematical definition of the model.
Although things like one-sided impacts or 0 impacts are often signs of problems with the model, if best fit values are at their boundary then they can only be one sided. And in some cases (e.g. positive-definite signal and background expectations and  0 observed events) it is also impossible for the background expectation to change the best fit value and therefore there will be no impacts whatsoever.
Its ultimately a physics decision, and not mine to make, but I don’t personally think there is sense in worrying about these impacts.
NOTE: Everything I say above is with regards to you 1600 GeV mass point, as from what I understand your background model is significantly different for different mass points; I have not looked at any others.
Best,
Kyle
