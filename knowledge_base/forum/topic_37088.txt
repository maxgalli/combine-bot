REPLY 4 (by fiemmi @ 2024-03-19T12:09:57.832Z):
Dear Sasha,
I agree with what Giacomo just said. In your second fit, r isn’t allowed to exceed 20 and this is why your “positive-side” error for width is reduced a lot.
To add on top of this, I believe this is expected. I don’t know the details of your analysis, but I think I can interpret r as the signal strength and width as some parameter (natural width?) that can make the peak of your signal distribution broader. Now, if you think in terms of PDFs, it turns out that r and width are positively correlated: if width increases, the peak gets broader and in order for the PDF normalization to stay ==1, the peak also gets “shorter”. To compensate for this, the fit will increase r to bring it “back up”.
Thus, if when you fit width you don’t allow r to take values that are large enough, you are basically artificially spoiling this correlation (because r can’t grow above 20).
BTW, --rMin and --rMax are setting boundaries on r. You can set ranges in a more general way by doing --setParameterRanges  r=-100.0,100.0:width=0.0,5.6.
I hope this helps, all the best,
Fabio.

REPLY 6 (by fiemmi @ 2024-03-19T12:27:21.148Z):
HI Sasha;
indeed the likelihood scan looks weird. My suggestion would be to set properly the parameter you want to scan (-P option), the ranges for all the POIs (--setParameterRanges option) and also to save the POIs that are “inactive” (in this case, r) during the scan (--saveInactivePOI 1 option) , so that you can check the correlation between r and width during your likelihood scan.
Something like:
combine -M MultiDimFit 2017UL_dimuon_SR2.txt -P width --setParameterRanges r=-100.0,100.0:width=0.0,5.0 --algo grid --points 100  --floatOtherPOIs=1 --saveInactivePOI 1 -v 3.
When the scan is over, you can open the output .root file and do:
limit->Draw("r:width")
and see what the correlation looks like.
I hope this helps, cheers,
F.
