QUESTION (by mohd @ 2026-04-02T12:46:08.398Z):
Dear all,
I’m reaching out regarding a fitting behavior we’ve encountered in our higgsino search in H(bb)H(γγ)+ptmiss channel. We’re using a 2D unbinned extended likelihood fit in M(γγ) and ptmiss as part of our background estimation strategy.
The fit works well in signal injection toy studies — convergence is clean and we are able to extract the right signal yield with no bias. The issue arises when no signal is injected (or in control region fits): the minimizer converges to a large negative n_sig, which we’ve verified is a true NLL minimum rather than a bug. The 1D projections at this minimum look visually poor, yet the fit prefers this solution over one with n_sig ≥ 0, which gives a visually reasonable result.
We traced this to the signal PDF being localized in a high-ptmiss region where data are sparse, resulting in minimal overlap between the signal PDF and the data distribution. I reproduced this behavior in a 1D toy example to isolate the effect (slides attached).
This raises a few related questions:
1. Is this a known pathology of extended likelihood fits in low-overlap regimes — i.e., a case where the NLL minimum is formally valid but physically uninterpretable, and the standard treatment is simply to impose a lower bound at n_sig = 0? We want to confirm this is the correct prescription rather than a sign of a deeper issue with the fit setup.
2. For the downstream limit setting in Combine, is it standard practice to set --rMin 0 to enforce the same physical boundary? Or does Combine’s internal profiling require r to float negative for the likelihood ratio test s

REPLY 2 (by gortona @ 2026-04-03T10:01:12.000Z):
Dear Mohd,
I’m not sure about question 1, and I would let people with more expertise answer on this point.
About question 2, in principle there is no objection to constraint the range of the r parameter in order to exclude unphysical or problematic regions of the phase space. For example, excluding r<0 is routinely done in analyses with large S/B to avoid the overall PDF to become negative.
One caveat about this approach is that Wilks’ theorem does not hold in the presence of a boundary. So if your final best fit is close to the forbidden region you can’t just take the DNLL=1/3.84 values as references and you might need to use toys to estimate the actual 68%/95% CLs.
I hope it helps,
Best,
Giacomo
