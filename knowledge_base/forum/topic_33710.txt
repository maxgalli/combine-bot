REPLY 2 (by kcormier @ 2024-01-30T05:34:44.845Z):
Dear Alexei,
@cousins may want to jump in for addition or in case I misstate something, since he is the author of the note. But as is mentioned in the note, its scope is about “a generalization of this usual chisquare to the case where the data are not gaussian.” That is a partial answer to your first question. The formula you cite is one that is constructed using  the assumption that the data are gaussian; the note is pointing out how the chi-squared can be derived from the likelihood ratio with the saturated model in this case. But, one of the advantages of the likelihood ratio is that it does not rely on this assumption, its just that in that case you get back the more familiar chi-squared. Combine uses the likelihood defined with poisson terms, not gaussian ones.
For question two, \sigma_i is not the uncertainty of the model, either pre-fit or post-fit. The relevant quantity for the saturated goodness of fit test is the likelihood of the parameters given the data, which is numerically equal to the probability of the data given the parameters.  For simplicity and definiteness, lets consider the Poisson likelihood since that is what combine actually uses for counts or histograms.  The likelihood terms for each bin which go into the ratio are Poisson, where the expectation is the central value of the model, and Poisson is evaluated for the actual number of observed data.
Note that this depends only on the expected value of the model at the point being evaluated and the observed data, the model uncertainties do not explicitly enter the calculation. The sigma from the note is taken by approximating the Poisson as a Gaussian, since its dealing with the Gaussian limit. But it still comes from the form of the likelihood directly and is not equal to the model uncertainties, except in special cases where the two may coincide.
To run the goodness of fit for the background-only case you can use the argument --fixedSignalStrength 0 which is special for the GoodnessOfFit method, but fixing and setting the signal strength to 0 in the ‘generic’ way that you have should also work.
For the last question, I wouldn’t say that one is agnostic to the signal strength. You are right that first a fit is performed to the data, then toys are generated in order to estimate the distribution of the saturated test statistic under the hypothesis that those best fit values are the ‘true’ parameter values. I’m not quite sure what you mean by ‘agnostic to the signal’, so I don’t know if that fully answers your question or not. But the Goodness of Fit will, in general depend on the best fit parameters, including the signal strength when looking at the signal+background model.
I hope that helps.
Best,
Kyle
