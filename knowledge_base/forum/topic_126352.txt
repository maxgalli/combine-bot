QUESTION (by mmadhu @ 2025-06-25T01:40:06.208Z):
Dear experts,
I’m trying to calculate median expected upper limits in a low statistics regime. When I use a simple counting experiment datacard with zero background (first attachment) and execute it in Combine v10.0.2 with the following options:
combineTool.py -M HybridNew --LHCmode LHC-limits --rMin 0 --rMax 50 --cl 0.90 -T 10000 --expectedFromGrid 0.5 --rRelAcc=0.001

I get a limit of 15.4252 (+/- 0.064595). This is expected as ln(1/0.1)/0.1495 = 15.40 for zero background.
However, when I introduce uncertainties on the signal and background like in the second attachment and run it with the same options, the limit becomes better (15.0571 +/- 0.0849267). I would have expected the limit to become worse after introducing uncertainties and to get a value higher than 15.40. Is there a way to get a more accurate value for the expected upper limit using HybridNew?
-Arun

REPLY 2 [ACCEPTED ANSWER] (by nckw @ 2025-06-25T08:22:05.320Z):
Hi Arun,
I think this is related to a known issue with the usual way we do limits, in that it can happen that an upper limit can improve when there is some background compared to no background (it happens due to the nature of the Poisson pdf and trying to calculate exact intervals).
In your case, in the first card, you have a non-zero background rate and this can be modified by the very small uncertainty.
Generally, our standard method for limit setting in combine is not suitable for zero or very close to zero expected background. Instead, you can use the approach described in this paper : https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0168900292907945?via%3Dihub (Cousins & Highland).
In combine, this can be achieved by following the instructions here : https://cms-analysis.github.io/HiggsAnalysis-CombinedLimit/latest/part3/commonstatsmethods/#computing-limits-with-toys (look for the term “Hybrid-Bayesian”).
Can you try with this method and see if the results look more like what you would expect?
Best,
Nick
