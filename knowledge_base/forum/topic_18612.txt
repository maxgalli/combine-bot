REPLY 6 (by agilbert @ 2022-12-14T15:48:26.404Z):
Hi,
The -P option is nothing to do with randomising POIs. In MultiDimFit it’s used to select a particular subset of the POIs to fit/scan (with other POIs fixed to their initial values unless --floatOtherPOIs 1 is also specified). When you generate toys you should find a distribution of fitted values for the POIs, but the values used to generate the toys are typically fixed to some particular hypothesis.
If you wanted to randomize the POIs with a uniform density you would still have to choose the range over which to do this, which seems somewhat arbitrary to me. Perhaps you could explain in some more detail what you want to do with this study, or maybe you have some slides with more information?
cheers,
Andrew

Hi Brent,
I haven’t looked at the paper you linked in full detail, but I’ve taken a quick look at it and at what you’re trying to do.
If I understand you correctly, you are trying to check the distribution of the log-likelihood ratio to see if it follows the asymptotic distribution predicted by Wilk’s theorem, because it has been pointed out by this paper that this will not be true in all cases (because the parameters may not lie on the interior of the model space, violating one of the theorem’s assumptions).
So what  I understand you’re trying to do is generate pseudoexperiments with fixed values for your model parameters, then check the distribution of the log-like likelihood ratio, to see if it is following the one predicted by Wilk’s theorem. Is that right? Then for this test, you would like to repeat it for each wilson coefficient, where you allow only that particular wilson coefficient to be a free parameter in the fit? Thus trying to test the Asymptotic distribution separately for each of the POIs?
It wasn’t clear to me if you then also planned to repeat this test at several points in the parameter space (as I think Andrew was suggesting)?
Did I understand that correctly?

Its true that the saturated test statistic’s distribution will depend on the number of bins (but also on the number of free parameters in the model). But it’s not clear to me why you are mentioning it here, maybe I’ve missed why you are bringing it up?  I thought you were always planning to test the log-likelihood ratio between a nominal model with all WCs fixed to the value used to generate the toys and a model with one WC floating.

Correct me if I’m wrong, but this command is varying ctp, then it compute the yields (as I mentioned above) and fit the WCs, and finally compares this fit to a fixed value.

The parameter of interest (in this case your wilson coefficient ctp) is not varied in the toy generation. the use of -P and --floatOtherPOIs are specifying what should be done in the fit, not in generating the toys.
In your case it might also be helpful to explicity separate the toy generation step from the fitting step by first running with -M GenerateOnly as described here [1].
This has two benefits that I can see. Firstly, you can re-use the same pseudoexperiment for different fits (e.g. where different WCs are floating or fixed). Secondly, it helps to clarify conceptually what is being done in the toy generation as opposed to the fit in terms of parameter values and settings.

The only question I have about this method is it is treating ctp different from the other WCs. Should we treat them all on equal footing, or do a fit like this one WC at a time?

Whether or not you want to treat them on the same or different footings depends on what you are trying to test and on what interpretations you will give. So I can’t answer that question without further discussion. Though if you want to provide more details I can give my two cents.  However, if you know which you’d like to do, we can certainly help ensure that what you are running is in fact doing what you intend.
One other thing to keep in mind which is relevant to this point is that the distribution expected from Wilk’s theorem depends on the additional number of degrees of freedom in the larger model, so you expect different distributions based on whether you allow the other WCs to float in the fit or keep them fixed.

Looking back at your last email, you mentioned fixed parameter points. Did you just mean using the fixed algo without toys, and just seeing how the test statistic changes as we vary ctp, basically like a grid scan?

The fixed algorithm for MultiDimFit  (described here [2]) means comparing the log-likelihood at a fixed point in parameter space as compared to the best fit point. Note that this doesn’t have anything to do with the data, or the pseudodata which is used for the fit, i.e. it is not specifying anything about how the (pseudo)data are being generated.
I think what Andrew was suggesting (he can correct me if I’ve misinterpreted) is that you can generate pseudodata (toys) for some fixed values of the parameters, and then perform your tests on those toys. One obvious example choice is generating pseudodata from the SM expectation (with all WCs set to 0). But it may be useful to also rerun the tests for other points in model space (e.g. ctp=1.5, all other WCs = 0), because the degree to which Wilk’s theorem is a good approximation will in general depend on where you are in parameter space.
I hope that helps,
Kyle
