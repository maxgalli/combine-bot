REPLY 3 (by amarini @ 2021-10-08T12:43:18.000Z):
*** Discussion title: Higgs Combination
Hi Roberto,
First considerations are the followings:
(1) you may have two different workspaces (the output of text2workspace) with the two models. One with r and the other with (r1, r2, ... rN)
(2) you can build a single workspace with the two models simultaneously inside, and  you may define the set of pois and the freezing or floating conditions at run time.
(3) you may have a single physics model, with 'r' and additionally n-1 orthogonal points (r'1, r'2, .. r'N-1).
I assume you did (2), since it is a bit more handy than (1). I also will highlight some sketch on possible expansions in the description using (3).
As you correctly said, you could run an asymptotic test using Wilks' theorem.
This can be achieved with:
(a)
combine -M MultiDimFit --saveWorkspace --redefineSignalPOIs r --freezeParameters r1,r2,rN --setParameters r1=1,r2=1,..,rN=1 -d (1) [...]
(b)
combine -M MultiDimFit -d (a) --snapshotName MultiDimFit --skipInitialFit --redefineSignalPOIs r1,r2,rN --freezeParameters r --setParameters r=1 [...]
and comparing the deltaNLL value in the last limit tree. Since the second fit could be re-arranged such that the N degrees of freedom describes the first fit, this is effectively a test of a chi-square with N-1 degrees of freedom (Wilks').
In principle the deltaNLL you are using above is the test statistics. You can check with toys (probably I would use the outcome of (a) to define toys frequentist postfit toys or simply repeat the fit as in (a) if it is not computationally expensive).
combine -M GenerateOnly --toysFrequentist --saveToys --snapshotName MultiDimFit -d (a) --bypassFrequentistFit [...]
and then fit them with (a) and (b) to obtain the distribution of deltaNLL.
In this case, you will get directly a p-value.
If you have constructed a workspace as in (3), previous steps can be obtain with a series of relaxations and enforcement of constraints, but ideally similar.
However, the model is explicitly nested. This could be used to redefine for instance the test-statistics. In the case in which N=2, which I understand is not your case but let's take this as an example, you can for instance compute also one sided confidence intervals using the CLs prescription and the modified test-statistics for upper limit. An other possibility for a generic N, would be to obtain FC confidence intervals on the r'1..r'N-1 1D (one floating) or N-1 D (all floating) together with r, and checking if the null hypothesis is within the established intervals.
Potentially, the asymptotic method should be fine in most of the cases, unless you are dealing with low counts. The verification with toys is feasible if the p-value to be estimated is not too small, otherwise a too large set may be needed.
Cheers,
-Andrea

Hi,

Here the issue lies in the treatment of theoretical uncertainties on the signal templates, which in the STXS fit we tweak to remove their normalization effect since we quote cross sections as a result.

This removal changes (which I agree for quoting xsecs) the uncertainties but not the best fit point.
Adding them back will make them strengths (i.e. ratio of obs/mc cross sections).
This is due to the fact that the free parameters will always be preferred for compensation wrt the constrained one at the minimum of the likelihood.
Can you try to put those back, rerunning the fit and check that the best-fit (observed) points don’t change?
You should be able to add them in the datacard, possibly as lnN, since these affects only processes and you already have them split.

REPLY 11 (by amarini @ 2021-12-10T12:41:14.000Z):
Hi Roberto,

The NLL is the negative log-likelihood.

The likelihood of the manyPOIs configuration should be always greater or equal than the likelihood of one-POI, thus the NLL_one >= NLL_many.
I don’t see the missing minus sign. I think we need to make sure that the two configurations lead to a compatible log-likelihood ratio.

Yes, it is correct. Wilks’ theorem states that the asymptotic distribution of the negative log-likelihood-ratio is a chi-square distribution with deltaParameters DoF, if the likelihood is well behaved (first and second derivatives exists), if you are not at the border of the parameter space in asymptotic conditions

and your model is truly sensitive to that many parameters (i.e., the likelihood is not flat in some direction).
This more or less happens in case of a “real” signal, while, for instance, a search with a floating mass will break these conditions (and thus have to deal with LEE).

The absolute value of the likelihood (nll) is kind of meaningless, and has a meaning only in “deltas” (i.e., in likelihood ratios). In many practical cases, it is also difficult to track it down correctly since many numerical minimization tools (including both RooFit and Combine), remove large constant offsets in performing the minimization.

For instance, in the Poisson likelihood, P(m| k) = exp(-m) m^k / k!, it is common practice to remove the large and difficult to compute factorial term (k!) that depends only on the observations.
Cheers,
-Andrea
